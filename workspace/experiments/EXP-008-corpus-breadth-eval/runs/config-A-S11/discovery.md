## [BLOCKER] — Privacy Act Assessment Required: Derived Data Consent Scope Not Assessed

The brief states that the privacy team has reviewed the overall consent model and is "comfortable with the approach." The follow-up context reveals that the review was conducted in the context of "sharing customer data with consent" — not in the context of "sharing derived inferences with consent." These are materially different processing activities under the Privacy Act 2020.

**The specific gap:** The enriched insights tier (spending category breakdowns, estimated monthly income, 90-day projected balance) are outputs of the enterprise's proprietary internal analytics engine applied to customer transaction data. These outputs are not raw data from the core banking system. They are inferences — the enterprise's analytical interpretations of the customer's transaction history. Under Privacy Act 2020 Principle 10 (use limitation):

> "An agency that holds personal information that was obtained in connection with one purpose shall not use the information for any other purpose unless [the individual has authorised that other use]."

The customer's raw transaction data was obtained in connection with operating their bank account. Using that transaction data as input to an analytics engine to produce spending category outputs, income estimates, and projected balances — and then sharing those analytical outputs with a third party — is a secondary use of the original personal information. Unless the customer's consent specifically and explicitly authorises sharing of the enterprise's analytical inferences (not just their raw transaction records), sharing the enriched insights tier under a "transaction data" consent is likely a use limitation breach under Principle 10.

The Privacy Act policy excerpt (S11-privacy-act-cdr-policy-excerpt.md, Principle 10 Commentary) is explicit: "Consent to share 'transaction data' is consent to share transaction data — it is not automatically consent to share the agency's proprietary interpretations of that transaction data."

The EA registry (OBCP-RISK-001, OBCP-SRC-003) independently confirms: "derived data consent boundary not assessed; Privacy Act assessment of derived-data consent scope not yet commissioned; OBCP-SRC-003 must not be enabled in production until this assessment is complete."

**The privacy team's comfort with the raw-data consent model does NOT extend to the derived-data consent boundary.** These are separate legal questions. The privacy team reviewed:
- ✅ Granular consent (per data type, per third party) — compliant with Privacy Act Principle 11 and CDR-equivalent standard
- ✅ Revocation and deletion workflow — compliant with CDR revocation standard
- ✅ 24-hour deletion obligation — requirement identified
- ❌ Whether enriched insights (derived inferences) fall within the scope of a "transaction data" consent — NOT reviewed

**Required action before enriched insights launch:**

1. Commission a dedicated Privacy Act assessment from Privacy Counsel on: (a) whether enriched insights constitute a distinct category of personal information under Privacy Act 2020 Principle 10; (b) whether a "transaction data" consent scope covers sharing the enterprise's analytical inferences; (c) what consent form language would be required to validly authorise sharing of derived inferences with third parties; and (d) whether the current consent form language ("spending summaries and financial projections prepared by the enterprise based on your account activity") constitutes adequate Principle 3 disclosure.
2. Gate the enriched insights feature (OBCP-SRC-003) with a feature flag that defaults to false. The flag must remain false until Privacy Counsel provides a written opinion confirming the consent basis is adequate.
3. Launch the raw data API (transaction history, balances, credit card summaries) without the enriched insights tier. This eliminates the consent scope risk from launch scope entirely.

**Effect on launch timeline:** The 10-partner launch within 6 months is achievable without enriched insights. The enriched insights tier becomes a post-launch capability once the Privacy Act assessment is complete and the consent form is updated to explicitly describe that analytical inferences will be shared.

## Problem Statement

The enterprise's open banking programme requires a compliant consent management API that enables accredited third parties to access customer financial data. The core challenge is building a consent architecture that satisfies Privacy Act 2020 obligations and the NZ Open Banking Framework (CDR-equivalent) at the field level — while correctly resolving which data types fall within a customer's consent scope.

The business opportunity is clear: enabling fintechs, comparison services, and financial aggregators to build on top of customer-permissioned data creates a platform ecosystem, increases customer value, and positions the enterprise in the NZ open banking landscape. The 10-partner launch within 6 months is a defined commercial milestone.

The regulatory problem is layered:
- **C1 (Privacy Act 2020):** Consent must be explicit, granular (per data type, per third-party recipient), time-limited, and revocable. The consent UI must meet Principle 3 disclosure requirements. The API must enforce Principle 10 use limitation at the field level.
- **C2 (CDR revocation and deletion):** On consent revocation, the third party must delete all received data within 24 hours. The enterprise must track deletion confirmation and escalate to DIA on late confirmation. The current plan has no technical enforcement mechanism — the 24-hour obligation cannot be demonstrated on request.
- **C3 (Per-call accreditation validation):** Only DIA-accredited third parties may receive data. Accreditation status must be validated on every API call, not just at onboarding. A suspended third party must be blocked immediately — not at next re-check.
- **C4 (Field-level data minimisation):** API responses must not include fields beyond what was explicitly consented. Minimisation applies at the field level, not just at the data-type level.
- **C5 [BLOCKER — Derived data consent scope]:** The enriched insights tier is derived data (analytical inferences), not raw transaction records. Whether sharing these inferences falls within the scope of a "transaction data" consent requires a dedicated Privacy Act assessment. The privacy team has not reviewed this question. Enriched insights must be excluded from launch scope until this assessment is complete.
