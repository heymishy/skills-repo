

# Discovery Report: Consent Management API for Open Banking

## Executive Summary

This discovery report analyses the proposed Consent Management API for the enterprise's open banking programme. The concept is well-formed and the team has clearly thought through many critical aspects — granular consent, real-time accreditation checks, and time-limited grants are all strong foundations. However, discovery has surfaced several areas requiring deeper investigation before committing to implementation scope and timeline, particularly around the enriched insights tier, the data deletion obligation, and the 6-month timeline with 10 partners at launch.

---

## What We Understand

### Core Concept
A consent-managed API enabling accredited third parties to access customer financial data, with customers exercising granular control over what is shared and with whom.

### Data Types in Scope
| Tier | Data Types | Source |
|------|-----------|--------|
| **Raw Banking Data** | Transaction history (up to 12 months), current account balances, available credit | Core banking system |
| **Credit Card Data** | Statement balances, payment due dates, utilisation | Core banking / card system |
| **Enriched Insights** | Spending category breakdowns, estimated monthly income, 90-day projected balance | Internal analytics engine |

### Consent Model
- Granular by data type and by third party
- Managed through the member portal
- Time-limited (up to 12 months)
- Revocable at any time
- Revocation triggers a deletion obligation on the third party

### Third-Party Accreditation
- Required before any data access
- Validated on every API call, not just at onboarding
- Loss of accreditation immediately blocks new data access

### Launch Target
- 10 accredited partners within 6 months
- Enriched insights available from day one

---

## What's Strong About This Approach

**Granular consent is the right model.** Allowing customers to choose both data types and specific third parties gives genuine control, not just a blanket "agree to everything" flow. This positions the enterprise well for regulatory expectations and customer trust.

**Real-time accreditation validation is a meaningful security posture.** Checking accreditation status on every call rather than relying on periodic sync or onboarding-time checks closes a real window of risk. This is a stronger position than many open banking implementations take.

**Time-limited consent with revocation rights** aligns well with Privacy Act principles around ongoing purpose limitation. The 12-month maximum is a reasonable ceiling.

**The privacy team's early involvement** in reviewing the consent model is a positive signal. Many programmes bring privacy in too late.

## Areas Requiring Further Investigation

### 1. Enriched Insights Carry Different Risk and Complexity Than Raw Data

**Why this matters:** The enriched insights tier (spending categories, estimated income, projected balance) is qualitatively different from the raw banking data. These are derived, inferential outputs from an analytics engine — they represent the enterprise's _interpretation_ of customer behaviour, not the customer's own data in the traditional sense.

**What we need to investigate:**

- **Privacy classification of derived data.** Has the privacy team specifically assessed whether enriched insights constitute "personal information" under the Privacy Act, and if so, whether the consent model needs to distinguish between sharing a customer's own data and sharing the enterprise's analytical inferences _about_ a customer? The statement that privacy reviewed "the overall consent model" may not have included this distinction at sufficient depth.

- **Accuracy obligations.** If a third party makes a lending or eligibility decision based on "estimated monthly income" or "projected balance," and that estimate is wrong, what is the enterprise's exposure? Under Privacy Principle 8 (accuracy), there may be an obligation to ensure data shared is accurate, up to date, and not misleading. Inferred data carries inherent uncertainty that raw transaction data does not. How will the API communicate confidence levels or caveats?

- **Competitive and commercial implications.** The enriched insights are generated by the enterprise's own analytics engine and are described as providing "significantly more value" than raw data. Has there been a commercial assessment of whether giving this away under the same consent framework as raw data appropriately values this intellectual property? Is there a different pricing or access tier intended? This isn't a blocker, but it's a strategic question that should be answered deliberately, not by default.

- **Analytics engine readiness.** Is the internal analytics engine currently designed to serve real-time or near-real-time API responses at the scale of 10 partners making concurrent calls? Enriched insights that are generated in batch for internal dashboards may not be straightforwardly exposable via a synchronous API without performance and freshness concerns.

**Recommendation:** Conduct a focused assessment with the privacy team specifically on enriched insights as a distinct data category. Separately, validate with the analytics engine team that insights can be served at API-appropriate latency and freshness. Consider whether enriched insights should be a fast-follow after launch rather than day-one scope, to reduce launch risk without abandoning the goal.

---

### 2. The Data Deletion Obligation Needs a Credible Enforcement Mechanism

**Why this matters:** The requirement that third parties must delete all data received under a consent upon revocation is a strong customer protection — but it is only as valuable as the enterprise's ability to verify compliance.

**What we need to investigate:**

- **How will deletion be verified?** Contractual obligation alone is insufficient for a programme of this nature. What mechanism will confirm that a third party has actually purged the data? Will there be attestation requirements, audit rights, technical verification (e.g., deletion confirmation callbacks), or periodic compliance audits?

- **What is the deletion timeline?** "Must delete" needs a specific SLA. Is it immediate? Within 24 hours? Within 30 days? Different timelines have very different technical and compliance implications for third parties. This needs to be defined explicitly in the API contract and the accreditation terms.

- **What about derived data at the third party's end?** If a third party received transaction history and used it to generate their own analytics, risk scores, or reports, does the deletion obligation extend to those derivatives? This is a known hard problem in data sharing frameworks and must be addressed in the contractual and technical design.

- **What happens to data already in flight?** If consent is revoked while a third party has a pending request, or has cached data for processing, how is that handled?

**Recommendation:** Design the deletion obligation as a first-class technical and contractual feature, not an afterthought. Define specific SLAs, build a deletion notification webhook into the API specification, require third-party attestation of deletion, and include audit rights in accreditation agreements. Consider whether the accreditation authority has a role in compliance monitoring.

---

### 3. Real-Time Accreditation Validation Needs Architectural Definition

**Why this matters:** Validating accreditation status on every API call is the right security posture, but it introduces a hard dependency on the accreditation authority's systems being available and responsive.

**What we need to investigate:**

- **What is the accreditation authority's system availability and latency?** If the authority's status-check endpoint goes down, does the entire API stop serving data? What is the fallback — deny all access (safe but disruptive) or use a cached status with a defined staleness window (more available but introduces risk)?

- **What is the expected call volume and can the accreditation check scale?** With 10 partners making potentially high-frequency calls, the accreditation validation endpoint could become a bottleneck or single point of failure.

- **Is there a webhook or push model available?** Rather than polling or synchronous checks, can the accreditation authority push status changes (particularly revocations), allowing the enterprise to maintain a local cache that is updated in near-real-time? This would be more resilient and performant.

**Recommendation:** Define the accreditation-check architecture explicitly. Establish an SLA with the accreditation authority. Design for the failure case — when the authority is unreachable, the system should fail closed (deny access) with appropriate monitoring and alerting. Investigate event-driven status updates as a complement to synchronous checks.

---

### 4. The Consent Model Needs Deeper UX and Edge Case Definition

**Why this matters:** The consent model is well-articulated at a policy level, but several interaction design and edge case questions will significantly affect both the member portal build and the API contract.

**What we need to investigate:**

- **Consent modification vs. revocation.** Can a customer narrow their consent (e.g., remove transaction history but keep balances) without fully revoking and re-granting? This affects the API's consent state model and the third party's data handling.

- **Consent renewal.** When a 12-month consent approaches expiry, is the customer prompted to renew? Is there a grace period? Or does access simply stop? The UX and third-party experience differ significantly depending on this choice.

- **Consent visibility and audit trail.** Can customers see a log of what data was actually accessed by each third party under their consent, or only what they consented to share? Access logs visible to customers build significantly more trust but require additional infrastructure.

- **Concurrent consents.** Can a customer grant consent to the same data type to multiple third parties simultaneously? Presumably yes, but the member portal UX needs to make this manageable and comprehensible.

- **Third-party naming and trust signals.** When a customer selects a third party in the consent flow, what information do they see? Just a name, or also accreditation status, a description of how the data will be used, and a link to the third party's privacy policy? The quality of consent depends heavily on the quality of information presented.

**Recommendation:** Run a focused UX discovery on the consent journey before finalising the API contract, as the API's consent state model must support whatever the UX requires. Define edge cases for modification, renewal, and multi-party consent explicitly.

---

### 5. The 6-Month Timeline With 10 Partners and Enriched Insights Is Ambitious

**Why this matters:** This is not a concern about ambition — it's a concern about whether the scope and timeline have been tested against the delivery reality.

**What we need to investigate:**

- **Partner onboarding at scale.** Onboarding 10 accredited partners is not just a technical integration task. Each partner will need API credentials, sandbox access, testing, accreditation validation integration, contractual agreements, deletion obligation agreements, and likely bespoke support during initial integration. Has the onboarding pipeline been scoped and resourced?

- **Sandbox and testing environment.** Third parties will need a non-production environment with realistic test data to build their integrations. Does this exist, or does it need to be built?

- **Support model for partners.** Who handles partner queries during integration and post-launch? Is there a developer relations function, or does this fall on the core engineering team?

- **Regulatory or legal review timelines.** Even if the privacy team is comfortable with the consent model, are there legal reviews needed for the accreditation agreements, data sharing contracts, deletion obligations, and liability frameworks? These often take longer than expected.

- **Dependency on the analytics engine team.** If enriched insights are in scope for day one, the analytics engine team is on the critical path. Have they committed to the timeline and the API-serving requirements?

**Recommendation:** Map the full delivery plan including non-engineering workstreams (legal, partnerships, accreditation authority coordination, UX, analytics engine). Stress-test the 6-month timeline against this complete picture. Consider a phased launch: raw banking data with a smaller partner cohort first, enriched insights and full partner cohort as a fast follow. This reduces day-one risk without materially changing the programme's ambition.

---

### 6. Scope of "Access" vs "Ongoing Sharing" Needs Clarification

**Why this matters:** The current description is ambiguous about a fundamental architectural question — is this API a pull model (third parties request data on demand, within consent bounds) or does it also support push/subscription models (ongoing data feeds while consent is active)?

**What we need to investigate:**

- **Pull-only or also push/streaming?** Some open banking use cases (e.g., financial aggregator dashboards) work best with event-driven updates or periodic pushes. Others (e.g., one-off credit assessments) are well-served by pull. The architecture, consent model complexity, and data deletion implications differ significantly between these.

- **Rate limiting and fair use.** In a pull model, how frequently can a third party request data under a single consent? Without rate limits, a third party could effectively create a continuous mirror of the customer's financial data, which may exceed the customer's reasonable expectation of what "sharing" means.

- **Data freshness guarantees.** When a third party pulls current account balances, how fresh is that data? Real-time from core banking, or a cached snapshot? The API contract needs to specify this to avoid third parties making decisions on stale data.

**Recommendation:** Define the access model explicitly. For launch, a pull-only model with defined rate limits and freshness guarantees is likely the most appropriate scope. Document data freshness and caching behaviour in the API specification.

## Key Assumptions to Validate

| # | Assumption | Risk if Wrong |
|---|-----------|---------------|
| 1 | The privacy team's review covered enriched insights as a distinct data category, not just the consent model generically | May need to revisit what's in scope for day-one launch; potential Privacy Act exposure on inferred data |
| 2 | The internal analytics engine can serve enriched insights at API-appropriate latency and scale | Enriched insights tier may not be technically feasible for day one without significant analytics platform work |
| 3 | The accreditation authority provides a reliable, low-latency status-check mechanism | Real-time accreditation validation may need a different architecture than assumed |
| 4 | Third-party deletion of data on consent revocation can be meaningfully verified | The deletion obligation may be a contractual fiction without enforcement mechanisms, undermining customer trust |
| 5 | 10 partners can be onboarded (legally, technically, operationally) within the 6-month window alongside building the API itself | Timeline may need to be phased to be achievable |
| 6 | The consent model is pull-only for launch | If any partners expect push/subscription models, the architecture and consent state management become significantly more complex |

---

## Suggested Next Steps

1. **Enriched insights deep-dive** — Convene the privacy team, analytics engine team, and commercial stakeholders to assess enriched insights as a distinct workstream. Answer: privacy classification, accuracy obligations, API-serving feasibility, and commercial model. This is the single highest-value investigation before committing scope.

2. **Deletion obligation design** — Task the technical architecture team with designing the deletion lifecycle, including notification mechanism, SLAs, attestation, and audit rights. Bring legal in to align contractual terms with technical design.

3. **Accreditation integration architecture** — Engage with the accreditation authority to understand their system capabilities, availability SLAs, and whether they support event-driven status updates. Design the failover model.

4. **Consent UX discovery** — Run a focused discovery on the member portal consent journey, covering modification, renewal, multi-party consent, and information presentation. Use this to stabilise the API's consent state model.

5. **Delivery plan stress test** — Map all workstreams (engineering, legal, partnerships, UX, analytics, accreditation authority coordination) onto the 6-month timeline. Identify the critical path and assess whether phased launch is appropriate.

6. **Define the API access model** — Explicitly decide pull vs. push, rate limiting, and data freshness guarantees. Document these as API design constraints before detailed specification begins.

---

## Summary Assessment

The programme's foundations are sound — granular consent, real-time accreditation validation, and revocable time-limited grants are the right building blocks. The primary risks are not in the concept but in **scope confidence** (enriched insights may carry more complexity than currently appreciated), **enforceability** (the deletion obligation needs a credible mechanism), and **timeline realism** (the full scope with 10 partners in 6 months requires validation against the complete delivery picture, not just the engineering build).

None of these are reasons to stop. All of them are reasons to investigate further before locking in commitments.
