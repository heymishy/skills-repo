# Discovery Report: Legacy Loan Ledger Migration

## Executive Summary

You are undertaking a time-constrained, high-stakes migration of 280,000 active loan accounts from an 18-year-old legacy system to your existing core banking platform. The hard deadline is vendor-imposed and non-negotiable. The primary risks are data integrity, regulatory compliance, and timeline execution. This report maps what you have told us, identifies what remains unknown, flags the risks that warrant immediate attention, and frames the decisions you need to make before detailed planning can begin.

---

## 1. What We Know

### 1.1 The Situation

| Parameter | Detail |
|---|---|
| Accounts in scope | 280,000 active loan accounts |
| Loan types | Personal loans, home loans, commercial lending |
| Legacy system age | 18 years in production |
| New platform status | 14 months live, deposit accounts only |
| Support contract end | 12 months from now — hard cutover deadline |
| Vendor contract extension | Not available; vendor withdrawing product |
| Team size | 6 engineers, 1 data architect, 1 project manager |

### 1.2 The Migration Architecture You Have Proposed

Your proposed timeline has three phases:

**Months 1–6 — Parallel Operation**
New loan originations go onto the new platform immediately. Existing accounts remain on the legacy system but all transactions are mirrored in real time to the new platform running in shadow mode.

**Month 6 — Validation and Cutover**
Data integrity validation and reconciliation pass. If satisfactory, staged weekend migration of remaining legacy accounts.

**Months 7–12 — Decommission**
Legacy infrastructure formally retired within the six months following cutover.

### 1.3 Data Requirements

Every loan record must migrate with full history:

- Original loan terms
- Drawdown history
- Repayment schedules
- Arrears status
- Associated security registrations

Closed loans are also in scope. Consumer finance legislation requires credit contract records to be retained for 7 years, so historical records cannot be discarded even after migration.

### 1.4 Regulatory Obligations

The new platform must reproduce all RBNZ regulatory reports currently generated by the legacy system. Report formats and field definitions are prescribed. Your RBNZ relationship team must validate that new platform output meets RBNZ requirements before cutover.

### 1.5 Tooling

The new platform vendor is providing a migration toolset. The assumption is that this toolset handles the primary extract, transform, and load process.

## 2. What We Do Not Yet Know

These are the open questions that carry the most planning risk. Several of them need answers before your month-by-month schedule can be built with any confidence.

### 2.1 Data and Systems

**Legacy data quality**
- Has any systematic data quality audit been run against the legacy loan records? Eighteen years of production data typically accumulates orphaned records, encoding inconsistencies, fields populated out of convention rather than specification, and business rule exceptions that were handled manually rather than through the system. You need to know the condition of the data before you can estimate the transformation effort.

- Are there closed loan accounts in the legacy system, and if so, how many? These still need to migrate for the 7-year retention obligation, but they add volume and may have different data structures.

- Were any data model changes made to the legacy system over its 18 years? Schema evolution in legacy systems is often poorly documented. Fields get repurposed, tables get added outside the original vendor schema, and some records may predate structural changes.

**System integrations**
- What other internal systems currently connect to the legacy loan ledger? Likely candidates include collections platforms, customer relationship management, document management, internet banking, broker portals, credit decisioning, and general ledger. Every integration point is a migration dependency.

- Are any of those integrations real-time? If collections or internet banking are calling the legacy system directly, the cutover sequence becomes more complex than a simple data migration.

- Does the legacy system feed your general ledger? If so, what happens to that feed during parallel operation, and how do you prevent double-counting?

**Security registrations**
- Where are security registrations actually stored? PPSR and mortgage registrations may live in external registries, a document management system, or a separate securities module rather than the loan ledger itself. The migration plan needs to account for wherever that data physically resides.

**The migration toolset**
- Has the vendor migration toolset been tested against your actual legacy data, or only against sample or synthetic data? The difference matters enormously. Toolsets built for generic migrations routinely encounter institution-specific edge cases that require custom transformation work.

- What does the toolset cover and what does it not cover? Specifically: does it handle the shadow mirroring architecture, or is that something your team builds separately?

- What does the toolset's reconciliation capability look like? Does it produce field-level reconciliation reports or only record counts?

### 2.2 Timeline and Capacity

**The parallel operation architecture**
- How will transaction mirroring work mechanically during the six-month parallel period? Is this event-based replication, batch synchronisation, or something else? The answer has direct implications for data consistency at cutover and for the complexity your engineers need to build and maintain.

- What is the source of truth during parallel operation for interest accrual, arrears status, and fee calculations? If the legacy system is authoritative and the new platform is receiving mirrored transactions, are you confident the new platform will apply the same business logic and arrive at identical balances? Divergence in shadow mode is the thing you most need to detect before cutover, not after.

- What triggers the decision to proceed or not proceed with cutover at month six? You have said "if results are satisfactory" but you have not yet defined what satisfactory means quantitatively. This needs to be defined in advance, not in the week before cutover.

**Weekend migration logistics**
- 280,000 accounts staged across a weekend — what does staged mean in your plan? Staged by loan type, by balance band, by region, by alphabetical cohort? The staging logic determines your rollback complexity if something goes wrong mid-weekend.

- What is your rollback plan if the weekend migration fails partway through? At what point does a partial migration become impossible to reverse cleanly?

- Have you modelled the actual data volumes and estimated migration run times? Weekend windows are typically 48 to 60 hours at most. You need to know whether your toolset can process 280,000 full-history records within that window.

**Team capacity**
- Six engineers, one data architect, and one project manager is a small team for a project of this scope and consequence. What are the engineers' current primary responsibilities? Are they being fully seconded to this project, or carrying other work in parallel?

- Does your team have prior experience with core banking data migrations, or is this a first for the organisation? The learning curve on banking data migrations is steep and the timeline does not accommodate extended ramp-up.

- The data architect — is this person available full-time for the duration, or are they shared with other programmes?

### 2.3 Regulatory

**RBNZ reports**
- How many distinct RBNZ reports does the legacy system currently generate? Are they all scheduled and documented, or have some become informal exports that are not in any official report catalogue?

- Do you have the formal field definitions and submission formats for every required report? If any of these exist only as tribal knowledge within the team that currently operates the legacy system, that knowledge needs to be extracted and documented before those people are redeployed.

- What is the RBNZ's process for certifying or accepting that a replacement system meets reporting requirements? Is there a formal approval process, or is it managed through your relationship team's confirmation? This matters for your timeline because regulatory approval cycles are not always within your control.

- Has the new platform vendor produced RBNZ-compliant report output for other New Zealand institutions? If yes, do those reports match your field definitions exactly, or will there be customisation work?

**Arrears and hardship reporting**
- Does the legacy system currently generate any hardship or arrears reporting beyond RBNZ submissions — for example, to your board, to credit bureaus, or under any industry codes? These need to be mapped to the new platform as well.

### 2.4 Legal and Compliance

**The 7-year retention obligation**
- Your legal team has confirmed the retention requirement, but have they confirmed *where* the records must be accessible from? Some regulatory interpretations require records to be retrievable from an active system rather than archived storage. This affects whether you can archive historical closed-loan records to cold storage after migration or whether they must remain queryable in the new platform.

- Are there records that predate the 7-year window that can be formally disposed of now? Reducing the migration scope where legally permissible is worth confirming.

**Credit contracts and variation records**
- Loan account records are not just balances. They include the original signed contract terms and any subsequent variation agreements. Are those documents stored in the legacy system, in a document management system, or in physical files? The migration plan needs to cover documents as well as data.

**Commercial loan complexity**
- Commercial lending accounts frequently carry bespoke terms, covenant schedules, and cross-collateralisation arrangements that do not map cleanly to standard loan data models. How many commercial accounts are in scope and how complex are they? This segment may require more manual validation effort than personal or home loans.

### 2.5 Operational

**Business continuity during parallel operation**
- What happens to loan servicing staff workflows during the six-month parallel period? Are they working in the legacy system only, or are they expected to begin familiarising themselves with the new platform during shadow mode?

- Who is responsible for investigating discrepancies identified during shadow reconciliation? This is ongoing operational work throughout the parallel period and it needs to be resourced, not just assigned to the project team as an afterthought.

**Customer communications**
- Do customers need to be notified of the migration? Consumer credit legislation in New Zealand may require notification of changes to how accounts are managed. Legal should confirm whether any notification obligation exists and if so what the timing requirements are.

## 3. Risk Register

The following risks are assessed at this stage of discovery. Ratings are indicative and should be formally reviewed once open questions above are answered.

### 3.1 Critical Risks

**R1 — Timeline is fixed and non-recoverable**
The 12-month deadline is hard. There is no vendor extension, no fallback. If the project slips, you operate an unsupported system processing 280,000 loan accounts with no security patches. This is not a theoretical risk — it is the baseline condition if the project fails. Every other risk on this register ultimately maps back to this one.

*Implication:* The project cannot afford to spend months one and two discovering problems that should have been identified in discovery. Data quality assessment, integration mapping, and toolset validation need to begin in week one.

**R2 — Unknown data quality in the legacy system**
Eighteen years of production data is almost certainly not clean. The extent of the problem is unknown. Data quality remediation is the most common cause of banking migration delays, and the remediation effort cannot be fully estimated until an audit is complete.

*Implication:* A data quality audit is the first critical path item. If significant remediation is needed, it competes directly with the six-month parallel operation window. You need to know the scale of the problem immediately.

**R3 — Shadow mirroring divergence not detected before cutover**
If the new platform applies different business logic to mirrored transactions — different interest accrual conventions, different fee calculation timing, different arrears staging rules — accounts will have divergent balances between the two systems at cutover. Detecting this in month five rather than month one means you have almost no time to diagnose and fix it.

*Implication:* Reconciliation during parallel operation cannot be a month-six activity. It needs to run continuously from day one, with automated alerts on divergence and a clear ownership and resolution process.

**R4 — RBNZ reporting not validated in time**
If the new platform's RBNZ report output is not confirmed to meet requirements before cutover, you either cut over to a system that may produce non-compliant reports, or you delay cutover. Neither is acceptable.

*Implication:* RBNZ reporting validation needs to begin as soon as the new platform has loan account data in it — which means early in parallel operation, not at the end. Your RBNZ relationship team needs to be engaged at project initiation, not as a downstream sign-off.

**R5 — Weekend migration window insufficient for volume**
280,000 full-history records is a substantial volume. If the migration toolset has not been benchmarked against your actual data volumes, you do not know whether the cutover weekend window is achievable. Discovering a 72-hour run time during a 48-hour window is a project-ending problem at that stage.

*Implication:* Volume and performance testing of the migration toolset against production-representative data is required well before the cutover date. This should be a formal milestone with pass/fail criteria.

### 3.2 High Risks

**R6 — Team capacity is thin for project scope**
Eight people, some of whom may be carrying other responsibilities, is a lean team for a project with this many workstreams running simultaneously: data quality remediation, integration repointing, shadow mode build and operation, continuous reconciliation, RBNZ report validation, staff training, and cutover execution. If one or two team members are unavailable for any sustained period, the critical path compresses further.

*Implication:* Team commitment and backfill arrangements need to be confirmed before the project starts. The project manager needs explicit authority to escalate resource conflicts.

**R7 — Integration repointing not fully mapped**
If downstream systems continue to call the legacy loan ledger after cutover, they break. The full integration map is not yet confirmed. Some integrations may require significant development work, particularly if they are calling legacy-specific APIs or consuming legacy-specific data formats.

*Implication:* Integration discovery is an early critical path item. Any integration that requires development work on a third-party system needs to be identified immediately so that vendor engagement and development can begin.

**R8 — Commercial loan data complexity**
Commercial lending accounts are often structurally more complex than retail accounts. Bespoke terms, covenant tracking, cross-collateralisation, and syndication arrangements may not map cleanly to the new platform's data model. The extent of this problem is unknown until the data is examined.

*Implication:* Commercial accounts warrant a dedicated data mapping exercise early in the project. If data model gaps are identified, the resolution options — platform configuration, custom fields, workarounds — need to be agreed with the new platform vendor before parallel operation begins.

**R9 — Security registration data location and completeness**
Security registrations are mentioned as in scope but their physical location is not confirmed. If mortgage and PPSR registrations are not in the loan ledger, they need to be sourced, mapped, and migrated from wherever they do reside. Incomplete security registration data on the new platform is a legal and credit risk exposure, not just a data quality issue.

**R10 — Rollback complexity after staged weekend migration**
A staged cutover of 280,000 accounts introduces the possibility of a mid-migration failure with some accounts on the new platform and some still on the legacy system. The rollback procedure in that scenario is complex and needs to be fully designed and tested in advance. An untested rollback plan is not a rollback plan.

### 3.3 Moderate Risks

**R11 — Staff readiness and training on loan servicing workflows**
Operations staff currently service loans in the legacy system. By cutover they need to be proficient in the new platform. Six months of parallel operation is an opportunity for familiarisation but only if training is deliberately structured. An unplanned approach risks having staff revert to workarounds or shadow-processing in spreadsheets after cutover.

**R12 — Customer notification obligations**
If consumer credit legislation requires customer notification of the migration and notification timelines are longer than anticipated, this could constrain the cutover schedule.

**R13 — Vendor migration toolset gaps**
Migration toolsets provided by platform vendors are built for common cases. Your data will contain uncommon cases accumulated over 18 years. The toolset gap analysis — what it handles, what it does not — needs to be done early so that custom transformation work can be scoped and built within the parallel operation window.
