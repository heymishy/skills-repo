# /discovery — Consent Management API: Open Banking Programme

## Discovery Report & Requirement Analysis

---

## 1. Executive Summary

This discovery covers the proposed Consent Management API that will enable accredited third parties to access customer financial data under explicit customer consent. The programme has a clear strategic intent, a reasonable architectural direction, and genuine privacy consciousness. However, discovery has surfaced several **material concerns** that must be resolved before build commences — particularly around the enriched insights tier, the consent model completeness, data deletion obligations, and timeline feasibility. This report organises findings, flags risks, raises open questions, and provides structured recommendations.

---

## 2. What Is Well-Defined

The following elements are sufficiently clear to begin detailed design without significant ambiguity:

| Area | Assessment |
|---|---|

## 3. Material Concerns Requiring Resolution

### 3.1 Enriched Insights — Significant Risk Area

This is the **highest-priority issue in discovery**. The enriched insights tier (spending category breakdowns, estimated monthly income, 90-day projected balance) is described as generated by an internal analytics engine, not sourced directly from core banking data. Several compounding risks attach to this.

**Privacy Act and consent scope risk:**
Customer consent will be described in terms of data types they recognise — their transactions, their balances. Enriched insights are *derived data* — inferences and predictions generated by the enterprise, not records of what the customer did. When a customer consents to share their data, it is not self-evident that they understand they are also consenting to share machine-generated assessments of their financial behaviour, income estimation, and forward-looking projections. Depending on jurisdiction and regulatory interpretation, derived and inferred data may carry distinct disclosure and consent requirements beyond the raw data they were derived from.

The statement that the privacy team is "comfortable with the approach" is noted, but discovery has not seen evidence that the privacy team reviewed the enriched insights tier *specifically* as a distinct data category with distinct consent language. This needs explicit confirmation.

**Consent notice risk:**
The consent notice shown to customers in the member portal must accurately describe what is being shared. "Spending category breakdowns, estimated monthly income, and 90-day projected balance" are materially different from "your transactions." If the consent notice conflates these or buries the distinction, consent may not meet the meaningful, informed standard required. This is not a hypothetical concern — regulators in comparable open banking regimes have taken action on inadequate consent notices for derived data.

**Third-party use risk:**
An estimated income figure or 90-day projected balance is potentially highly sensitive and analytically powerful. Third parties receiving this data could use it for automated decisioning (credit, insurance pricing, product targeting) in ways customers may not anticipate. If a third party's use of enriched insights leads to adverse customer outcomes, the enterprise will face questions about whether the consent collected was genuinely informed. There is also a question of whether the enterprise, as the entity generating and sharing these inferences, takes on analytical liability that does not exist when sharing raw transaction records.

**Data accuracy and liability risk:**
These are estimates and predictions, not facts. The 90-day projected balance, in particular, is a model output that can be materially wrong. If a third party relies on this figure — for a lending decision, for instance — and it is wrong, the enterprise has a potential liability exposure that does not exist for raw factual data. There needs to be a defined accuracy/disclaimer framework and a decision on whether the API response metadata communicates the probabilistic nature of these fields.

**Recommendation:** Do not include enriched insights in the launch scope until the following are resolved:
- Explicit privacy team sign-off specifically on derived/inferred data categories with documented rationale
- Consent notice language reviewed by a privacy specialist and, ideally, customer-tested for comprehension
- Legal advice on derived data liability and whether analytical outputs require additional disclosure obligations
- A defined accuracy/disclaimer framework and API metadata approach for probabilistic fields
- A decision on whether third-party permitted use cases for enriched insights need to be contractually constrained at accreditation

If after resolution the programme chooses to proceed with enriched insights at launch, this decision should be documented as a risk acceptance with named accountable owner and the mitigations listed above in place.

### 3.2 Consent Revocation and Deletion — Incomplete Specification

The requirement states that on revocation the third party must delete all data received under that consent. This is the right obligation to impose. However, discovery has not identified:

- **How deletion will be verified.** The enterprise cannot accept third-party self-declaration as sufficient. What is the verification mechanism — contractual attestation, audit rights, technical enforcement?
- **What the deletion timeframe is.** "Must delete" needs a defined SLA. Industry practice typically specifies 5–30 business days. The timeframe needs to be set, communicated to third parties at accreditation, and enforced.
- **What "all data" means in practice.** Does deletion extend to derived data the third party has themselves generated from the shared data — their own analytical outputs, models trained on the data, cached copies in downstream systems? This is a genuinely hard problem and needs a defined scope position.
- **What happens to data in transit or in processing pipelines at the time of revocation.** Is there a grace period? What is the API's behaviour for requests that are in-flight at the moment consent is revoked?
- **Whether the enterprise has audit or inspection rights.** If a third party does not comply with deletion, what is the enforcement path? This needs to be in accreditation agreements.

**Recommendation:** Define a complete deletion obligation framework before finalising third-party accreditation agreements. This should include timeframe, scope of "all data," verification mechanism, audit rights, and consequences of non-compliance. Engage legal prior to issuing accreditation agreement drafts.

---

### 3.3 Accreditation Validation on Every API Call — Design Gaps

The intent to validate accreditation status on every API call is a strong security posture. However, several design questions are unresolved:

- **Where does the accreditation authority's status register live, and what is the latency of updates?** If a third party loses accreditation and the register update takes 24 hours to propagate, there is a window of exposure. What is the acceptable maximum lag?
- **What is the failure mode if the accreditation authority's service is unavailable?** Fail-open (allow the call) or fail-closed (deny the call)? This is a security-critical decision. Fail-open is dangerous. Fail-closed could create availability issues for legitimate third parties. A time-bounded cached status with defined TTL may be the right approach, but it needs an explicit decision.
- **Who operates the accreditation authority register?** Is this the enterprise itself, a regulatory body, an industry scheme? The answer affects latency, reliability, and what the enterprise can control.
- **What does "blocked from new data access immediately" mean operationally?** Are existing in-flight requests completed? Are active sessions terminated? What notification, if any, is given to the third party?

**Recommendation:** Produce a detailed accreditation validation architecture decision record (ADR) covering: register location and ownership, update latency SLA, cache strategy and TTL, failure mode decisions, and blocked-party behaviour. This should be reviewed by the security team before API design is finalised.

### 3.4 Data Minimisation — Transactions Up to 12 Months

The specification states transaction history can be shared up to 12 months. This is a maximum, not a default. Discovery has not confirmed:

- Whether customers can select a shorter period as part of granular consent, or whether 12 months is presented as a binary on/off.
- Whether the API enforces the consented period at query time, or whether it is up to the third party to constrain their request.
- What happens if a third party requests 12 months of data but the customer consented to 3 months. Is this rejected at the API layer with an appropriate error, or is it on the third party to self-limit?

Privacy Act data minimisation obligations require that only data necessary for the consented purpose is shared. If the consent record specifies a shorter period and the API allows a longer pull, this is a compliance breach regardless of third-party intent.

**Recommendation:** Confirm that consent records capture the specific period consented to (not just a binary flag) and that the API enforces this at the data retrieval layer — not relying on third-party self-limitation.

---

### 3.5 Consent UI/UX — Scope and Ownership Unclear

Consent is collected through the member portal. Discovery has not confirmed:

- Whether the member portal team is in scope for this programme or whether the API team is providing a consent API that the portal will consume.
- Who owns the consent notice language and how it will be reviewed.
- How customers are informed when consent is about to expire (approaching 12-month limit) and what the renewal flow is.
- How customers discover which third parties currently hold active consent and can review/revoke them — what is the consent management dashboard experience?
- What accessibility standards apply to the consent UI.
- What the consent flow looks like on mobile versus desktop — is there a member app in scope?

The consent UI is not a minor integration point. It is the mechanism by which the legal and ethical validity of every data share is established. Poorly designed consent UI undermines the entire compliance position of the programme.

**Recommendation:** Confirm that member portal/UX is in scope and that a dedicated UX designer and content designer with privacy literacy are assigned to the consent flow. The consent notice language should go through privacy review and customer comprehension testing before the programme goes to user acceptance testing.

## 4. Open Questions for Stakeholders

The following questions must be answered before requirements are baselined. They are organised by audience.

### For the Privacy / Legal Team

1. Has the privacy team specifically reviewed the enriched insights tier as a distinct derived-data category? Can we see the documented sign-off?
2. What is the enterprise's legal position on derived and inferred data under the applicable Privacy Act? Does sharing analytical outputs require separate consent disclosure from sharing raw data?
3. What liability does the enterprise carry if a third party makes an adverse decision against a customer based on an inaccurate enriched insight (e.g., a materially wrong income estimate)?
4. Does the deletion obligation on revocation extend to third-party derivatives — data the third party has generated from our shared data?
5. Are there any sector-specific regulations (beyond the Privacy Act) that apply — for example, credit reporting obligations, anti-discrimination rules relevant to income estimation?
6. What audit rights does the enterprise need over third parties to demonstrate Privacy Act compliance to a regulator?

### For the Product / Programme Owner

7. Who operates the accreditation authority and what SLAs do we have for accreditation status updates?
8. Is the enriched insights tier a hard requirement for launch, or is it a desired feature? What is the business consequence of deferring it to a subsequent release?
9. What is the consent model for customers who granted consent before enriched insights was added as a data type — do they need to re-consent for that tier specifically?
10. What is the renewal journey for expiring consent? Is auto-renewal in scope?
11. Is the consent management dashboard (customer view of active consents and revocation) part of this programme or a dependency on another team?

### For the Engineering / Architecture Team

12. What is the source of truth for consent records — is there a consent service, or will consent be stored in the member portal's data store?
13. How does the API consume consent state at query time — direct DB read, event-driven consent cache, or a consent service API call?
14. What is the strategy for consent record auditability — do we need an immutable consent event log for regulatory purposes?
15. How will the API handle partial consent — for example, a customer consents to balance data but not transaction history, and a third party requests both in one call? Split response or rejection?
16. What is the expected API call volume from 10 launch partners, and does accreditation validation on every call create a performance bottleneck at that scale?
17. Is there an existing API gateway that handles authentication and rate limiting, or does this need to be built?

### For the Security Team

18. What is the agreed failure mode for accreditation validation when the accreditation register is unavailable?
19. What is the maximum acceptable accreditation status cache TTL?
20. What is the threat model for a third party that loses accreditation but attempts to continue calling the API using previously issued credentials?
21. What data residency and encryption requirements apply to the data in transit and at rest within the API infrastructure?

## 5. Proposed Scope Boundaries

Based on discovery, the following scope definition is recommended for baseline:

### In Scope — Core Launch (6-Month Target)

- Consent grant API (customers grant consent for specific data types and specific third parties, with defined duration)
- Consent revocation API (customers revoke consent; triggers deletion obligation notification to third party)
- Consent read API (third parties query whether active consent exists before requesting data)
- Data exposure APIs for: transaction history, current account balances and available credit, credit card summaries
- Accreditation validation on every API call
- Consent record storage with full audit log
- Third-party onboarding and credential issuance process
- Deletion notification mechanism to third parties on revocation
- Member portal consent UI (grant, view active consents, revoke) — *confirming this is in scope*

### Conditional on Issue Resolution — Enriched Insights

- Enriched insights tier (spending categories, income estimate, 90-day projection)
- Proceed only after privacy sign-off on derived data, consent notice review, liability framework, and accuracy/disclaimer approach
- If not resolved within the programme timeline, defer to a post-launch release — do not hold the core launch

### Out of Scope (Explicitly)

- Third-party accreditation process itself (assumed to be operated by the designated accreditation authority)
- Customer identity verification for portal access (assumed handled by existing member portal authentication)
- Third-party developer portal / API documentation portal (dependency, needs separate confirmation)
- Consent renewal / re-consent flows (recommend scoping for post-launch unless timeline permits)
