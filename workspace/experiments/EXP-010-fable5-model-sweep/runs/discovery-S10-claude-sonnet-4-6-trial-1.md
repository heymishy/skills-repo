# Discovery Report: Legacy Loan Ledger Migration

## Executive Summary

This report documents the discovery findings for the migration of 280,000 active loan accounts from the legacy loan ledger system to the existing core banking platform currently operating deposit accounts. The project operates under a hard 12-month deadline imposed by vendor end-of-support, with a planned 6-month parallel operation period followed by staged cutover and a 6-month decommission window.

The migration is technically feasible within the stated timeline. However, the combination of hard regulatory deadlines, legal data retention obligations, RBNZ reporting continuity requirements, and the complexity of three distinct loan product types across 18 years of accumulated records means this project carries material execution risk. That risk is manageable but requires early resolution of several open questions and a discipline around scope that the team will need to maintain under schedule pressure.

---

## Scope Definition

### What Is In Scope

- All 280,000 active loan accounts across personal loans, home loans, and commercial lending
- Full loan history for each account: original terms, drawdown history, repayment schedules, arrears status, and associated security registrations
- Closed loan records within the 7-year retention window mandated under applicable consumer finance legislation
- All RBNZ regulatory reports currently generated by the legacy system, reproduced to the same format and field definitions on the new platform
- The parallel operation and shadow mirroring infrastructure required during the 6-month transition period
- Staged weekend cutover of remaining legacy accounts at the 6-month mark
- Legacy infrastructure decommission within the final 6-month window

### What Is Out of Scope

- New loan origination workflow changes — new originations go onto the new platform from day one but origination process redesign is not part of this project
- Deposit account migration — already completed and in stable operation
- Closed loan records outside the 7-year retention window — these require a defined archival disposition but are not being migrated to the active platform
- Any enhancement to RBNZ report content or format — the objective is parity with current output, not improvement
- Core banking platform feature development — the platform is treated as fixed; this project adapts data to fit the platform, not the reverse

### A Note on Scope Discipline

Eighteen-year-old production systems accumulate organic complexity. There will be discovery of edge cases, unusual account configurations, and legacy workarounds that someone will suggest resolving as part of this migration. The team needs a clear and enforced decision rule: if a change is not required to achieve a valid, compliant migration of the loan records, it is deferred. Scope additions at any point after the first six weeks will threaten the cutover deadline.

## Timeline Analysis

### The Constraint Structure

The 12-month hard deadline is not a negotiating position — it is defined by the vendor's product withdrawal and confirmed as non-extendable. This means the timeline has no float at the back end. Every month of delay in starting clean work compresses the parallel operation period, the validation window, or both.

The planned structure is sound:

| Phase | Duration | Key Events |
|---|---|---|
| Preparation and parallel setup | Months 1–2 | Schema mapping complete, shadow mirroring live, data extraction tooling validated |
| Parallel operation | Months 1–6 | All new originations on new platform; legacy accounts mirrored to new platform in shadow mode |
| Data integrity validation | Month 6 | Full reconciliation pass; go/no-go decision for cutover |
| Staged weekend cutover | Month 6 | Remaining legacy accounts migrated in batches |
| Decommission | Months 7–12 | Legacy infrastructure retired; compliance archiving complete |

### Where the Timeline Is Vulnerable

**The parallel period requires shadow mirroring to be live from day one.** If mirroring infrastructure is not operational at the start of month one, the parallel period is effectively shorter than six months, which compresses validation time. Shadow mirroring setup must be the first completed deliverable, not something finished alongside other preparation work.

**The go/no-go decision at month six is a real decision point.** If reconciliation reveals data integrity issues that cannot be resolved in the cutover weekend, the team needs a contingency. The most realistic contingency is a short extension of parallel operation — but this consumes time from the decommission window and cannot be extended indefinitely. The contingency plan needs to define exactly how many additional weeks could be absorbed before the decommission window is fatally compressed.

**The decommission window is not passive time.** Retiring 18-year-old infrastructure involves more than switching off servers. There will be dependent integrations, archived media, compliance documentation, and likely some manual process that touches the legacy system in ways not captured in any architecture diagram. Six months for decommission is adequate if discovery of those dependencies starts now, not at month seven.

## Data Migration Risk Assessment

### Primary Risk: Data Integrity Across 18 Years of Accumulated Complexity

An 18-year production system will contain data that does not conform to any documented schema. This is not a criticism of the legacy system — it is an observation about the lifecycle of any long-running financial system. Staff changes, emergency patches, vendor workarounds, regulatory changes, and one-off customer accommodations produce records that are technically valid in the system that created them and technically problematic for any system attempting to consume them.

The specific concerns for this loan portfolio are:

**Personal loans** are the most structurally uniform of the three product types but will have the highest volume of edge cases in arrears history — payment arrangements, hardship variations, and partial write-offs that may be represented differently across the 18-year period as policies and system versions changed.

**Home loans** carry security registrations that must migrate intact. A home loan record where the security registration data is incomplete or mislinked is not a minor data quality issue — it affects the bank's legal position as a secured creditor. Security registration data requires explicit validation as a separate stream, not as part of the general reconciliation pass.

**Commercial lending** is the highest structural risk. Commercial loan facilities frequently involve complex drawdown arrangements, multiple tranches, revolving credit components, and covenant tracking. The data model for commercial lending in an 18-year-old system may not map cleanly to the new platform's schema. This is the area most likely to require custom transformation logic and the area most likely to surface surprises late in the project if not investigated early.

### Data Audit Recommendation

Before schema mapping begins, the team should run a data profiling exercise against the full legacy dataset. This is not the same as schema documentation — it is an empirical examination of what values actually exist in the database, including nulls, outliers, values outside documented ranges, and referential integrity violations. The output of this exercise will determine how much transformation logic is required and where manual remediation is needed before automated migration can proceed.

This exercise should be completed within the first three weeks. Its findings will materially affect the project's risk profile and may require the project manager to have a frank conversation with the steering committee about resourcing or timeline if the findings are significantly worse than anticipated.

### Closed Loan Records and the 7-Year Retention Requirement

The legal team has confirmed a 7-year retention obligation. This creates a category of records that must be preserved but do not need to be operationally active on the new platform. The migration plan should explicitly distinguish between:

- **Active accounts** — migrated to the new platform with full operational capability
- **Closed accounts within the 7-year window** — migrated to the new platform in a read-only or archived state, or preserved in a separately maintained compliant archive
- **Closed accounts outside the 7-year window** — require a documented disposition decision; they cannot simply be deleted without confirming no other legal hold applies

The legal team needs to confirm whether the 7-year clock runs from the date the account was closed or from the date of the last transaction. For accounts closed in the early years of the legacy system, this distinction matters for determining which records fall within scope.

A practical recommendation is to over-include rather than under-include. The cost of migrating records that turn out to be outside the retention window is low. The cost of discovering a missing record during a regulatory examination or litigation is high.

## RBNZ Regulatory Reporting

### The Requirement

The new platform must produce every RBNZ report currently generated by the legacy system, in the prescribed format and with the correct field definitions. This is not an area where approximation or close-enough is acceptable. RBNZ reporting is a regulatory obligation and format deviations will be identified during examination.

### The Risk

RBNZ reports are typically generated from aggregations of underlying loan data. If the data model of the new platform uses different field names, different calculation methods for derived values, or different categorisation logic for loan types, the report outputs will diverge from current outputs even if the underlying loan data is correctly migrated. This is a data transformation problem, not just a data migration problem.

The specific risk is that the new platform's native reporting produces numbers that look plausible but are calculated differently than the legacy system. This kind of divergence is difficult to detect without running both systems simultaneously and comparing report outputs — which is, in fact, exactly what the parallel operation period allows. RBNZ report comparison should be a formal part of the parallel operation validation programme, not an afterthought.

### RBNZ Engagement

The brief notes that the RBNZ relationship team will need to confirm that new platform reports meet requirements. This engagement needs to be initiated in month one, not month five. The reasons are:

- RBNZ may have questions or requirements that take time to resolve
- Any required adjustments to report generation logic need development time built into the project schedule
- RBNZ confirmation before cutover provides a documented compliance position that protects the bank in the event of any post-migration question about report accuracy

The RBNZ relationship team should be asked to provide a complete list of affected reports, confirm the current submission schedule for each, and identify the point of contact at RBNZ who would need to be notified of the system change. Some regulators require advance notification of material system changes — this should be confirmed and if required, the notification timeline should be added to the project plan.

## Team and Resourcing Assessment

### Current Proposed Team

The proposed team is six engineers, one data architect, and a project manager. This is a lean team for a migration of this complexity and this data volume within this timeline.

The team is appropriately structured if the scope is tightly controlled and the vendor migration toolset performs as anticipated. The data architect is correctly identified as a distinct role — this migration will produce data architecture decisions that have long-term consequences for the loan portfolio on the new platform, and those decisions need someone with the seniority and focus to own them.

### Resourcing Observations

**The vendor migration toolset is an assumption that needs to be tested immediately.** The brief notes that the new platform vendor is providing a migration toolset. What this toolset can and cannot do, what data transformations it handles natively versus what requires custom development, and what its performance characteristics are at the scale of 280,000 accounts with full history — these are not known from the brief. The first engineering task should be a structured evaluation of the vendor toolset against a representative sample of the actual legacy data, including edge cases from the commercial lending portfolio.

**Six engineers will need to cover distinct workstreams simultaneously.** At peak the team will be managing shadow mirroring operations, data transformation development, RBNZ report validation, security registration data validation, and cutover preparation at the same time. The project manager should map specific engineers to specific workstreams early and monitor for cross-workstream dependencies that create bottlenecks on individual team members.

**Business knowledge is a gap that needs to be addressed explicitly.** The brief does not mention subject matter experts from the lending business. The data architect and engineers can map schemas and validate record counts, but interpreting whether a particular arrears history configuration is correct, whether a commercial loan's drawdown structure is accurately represented, or whether a home loan's security registration is complete — these judgements require someone who understands loan products operationally. The project should have a named point of contact in the lending operations team who is allocated time to support migration queries, not just consulted ad hoc when an engineer finds something they cannot interpret.

## Open Questions Requiring Resolution

The following questions are unresolved from the information provided. Each requires a decision or confirmation before the corresponding project workstream can proceed with confidence.

**1. What is the current state of the legacy system's data quality?**
No data profiling has been completed. Until this is done, estimates of transformation effort and manual remediation volume are speculative. *Owner: Data Architect. Target: Week 3.*

**2. What does the vendor migration toolset actually handle?**
The toolset is referenced but its capabilities against this specific data are untested. *Owner: Lead Engineer. Target: Week 2.*

**3. How are closed loan records within the 7-year window being preserved?**
The migration plan should specify whether these are migrated as archived records on the new platform or held in a separate compliant archive. *Owner: Data Architect and Legal. Target: Week 4.*

**4. Does the 7-year retention clock run from account closure date or last transaction date?**
This affects which closed accounts are in scope. *Owner: Legal. Target: Week 2.*

**5. Which RBNZ reports are generated by the legacy system and what is the full field mapping?**
A complete report inventory is required before RBNZ report validation work can be planned. *Owner: RBNZ Relationship Team. Target: Week 3.*

**6. Does RBNZ require advance notification of a material system change affecting regulatory reporting?**
If yes, this notification may need to be made in the next 30 days to comply with any notice period. *Owner: RBNZ Relationship Team. Target: Week 1.*

**7. What is the commercial lending data model in the legacy system and how does it map to the new platform?**
Commercial lending is the highest structural risk. A preliminary mapping review should be completed before parallel operation begins. *Owner: Data Architect and Vendor. Target: Week 6.*

**8. What integrations with other systems currently consume data from the legacy loan ledger?**
Downstream dependencies — credit risk systems, customer relationship platforms, collections systems, general ledger feeds — need to be identified and their cutover dependencies planned. *Owner: Project Manager and Engineering Lead. Target: Week 4.*

**9. What is the contingency if the month-six reconciliation fails the go/no-go threshold?**
The project plan needs a defined contingency that specifies how many additional weeks can be absorbed and at what point the project escalates to the steering committee. *Owner: Project Manager. Target: Week 6.*

**10. Who is the lending operations subject matter expert allocated to support this project?**
A named business contact with committed time allocation is required. *Owner: Project Manager and Lending Operations leadership. Target: Week 1.*

## Recommended Immediate Actions

Listed in priority order for the first two weeks:

1. **Initiate RBNZ relationship team engagement** to determine notification obligations and begin report inventory. Any regulatory notice period starts running from this conversation.

2. **Run vendor migration toolset evaluation** against a representative sample of legacy data including commercial loan edge cases. This is the fastest way to validate or challenge the current resourcing and timeline assumptions.

3. **Confirm legal position on closed loan records** — retention window calculation method and disposition plan for out-of-scope closed records.

4. **Name the lending operations subject matter expert** and confirm their time allocation to the project.

5. **Begin the data profiling exercise** on the legacy system. Prioritise commercial lending data model analysis as the highest-risk product type.

6. **Map all downstream integrations** that currently consume data from the legacy loan ledger so cutover planning can account for each dependency.
