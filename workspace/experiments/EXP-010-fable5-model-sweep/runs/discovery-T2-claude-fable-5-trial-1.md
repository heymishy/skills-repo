# Discovery Pipeline — Step 1: Framing the Problem

Thanks — that's a solid starting point, but it's currently a **solution-shaped goal** ("improve onboarding") rather than a defined problem. Before we explore solutions, I need to ground this in evidence and scope. Let's sharpen it.

## What I heard
> New business customers have an onboarding experience that someone believes is underperforming.

## What's missing
I can't yet tell **what's broken, for whom, or how we'd know we fixed it.** A few key unknowns:

**1. Evidence — Why do you believe onboarding needs improving?**
- Specific signals (drop-off metrics, churn, support tickets, NPS/CSAT, sales feedback)?
- Or is this a hunch / executive directive / competitive pressure?

**2. The customer — Who exactly is "a new business customer"?**
- Company size (SMB vs. mid-market vs. enterprise)?
- Self-serve signup or sales-assisted?
- Is the struggling user the buyer, the admin, or end users on the team?

**3. The current experience — What does onboarding look like today?**
- Where does it start and end in your view? (Signup → first value? Contract → full rollout?)
- Where do you *suspect* people get stuck?

**4. Success — What outcome would make this project a win?**
- Activation rate, time-to-first-value, reduced support load, retention, expansion?
- Is there a target number or baseline?

**5. Constraints**
- Timeline, team capacity, anything already decided or off the table?

---

**You don't need to answer everything.** Even rough answers to **#1, #2, and #4** will let me draft a problem statement and hypotheses for the next step.

If you genuinely don't know yet — that's useful too. We'd then make the first discovery activity *gathering that evidence* (e.g., funnel analysis, support ticket review, 5 customer interviews) rather than guessing at solutions.
