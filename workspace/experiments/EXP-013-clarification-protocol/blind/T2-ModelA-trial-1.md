# Discovery Pipeline — Step 1: Framing the Problem

Thanks — that's a solid starting point, but it's currently an **opportunity statement, not yet a discovery-ready problem**. "Improve onboarding" describes a solution space; discovery works best when we anchor on evidence of a problem first. Before I generate hypotheses and a research plan, I need to fill some gaps.

## Quick clarifying questions

**1. Context**
- What's the product, and what does "onboarding" span today? (Signup → first login? Contract signed → first value delivered? Something else?)
- Who are these business customers — SMBs self-serving, or enterprises with sales-assisted onboarding?

**2. Evidence — why do you believe onboarding needs improving?**
- What signal triggered this? (e.g., low activation rates, early churn, support ticket volume, sales/CS feedback, NPS comments, a competitor comparison)
- Do you have any numbers? Even rough ones — "40% never complete setup" is far more useful than "it feels clunky."

**3. Outcome**
- What would success look like in 6 months? (Faster time-to-first-value? Higher trial-to-paid conversion? Fewer onboarding support tickets?)
- Is there a metric you're accountable for?

**4. Constraints & scope**
- Any constraints I should respect (timeline, engineering capacity, can't change pricing/contracts, etc.)?
- Anything already tried or explicitly off the table?

## What happens next

Once you answer (even partially), I'll proceed through the pipeline:

1. **Reframed problem statement** — outcome-oriented, evidence-based
2. **Assumption & hypothesis map** — what we believe vs. what we know
3. **Research plan** — who to talk to, what to ask, what data to pull
4. **Opportunity sizing & prioritization** — where to focus first

---

👉 **Answer whichever questions you can.** If you don't have data yet, say so — "we don't know" is a valid answer and becomes part of the research plan.
