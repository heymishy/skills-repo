# Discovery Pipeline — Step 1: Framing the Problem

Thanks — that's a solid starting point, but it's currently framed as a **solution direction** ("improve onboarding") rather than a **problem**. Before we go further, I need to understand what's actually broken or what opportunity you're chasing. Otherwise we risk optimizing onboarding when the real issue lives elsewhere (e.g., expectation-setting in sales, product complexity, or activation incentives).

Let's sharpen it. Please answer what you can — partial answers are fine, and "I don't know" is a useful answer too (it tells us where discovery research is needed).

## 1. Evidence — Why do you believe onboarding is a problem?
- What signal triggered this? (Churn in first 90 days? Support tickets? NPS/CSAT? Sales feedback? Executive intuition?)
- Do you have any numbers? e.g., time-to-first-value, activation rate, % of new accounts active at 30/60/90 days.

## 2. The customer — Who exactly is onboarding?
- What does "business customer" mean here — SMB self-serve, mid-market, enterprise with implementation teams?
- Who inside the customer's org goes through onboarding: an admin, end users, both?

## 3. Current state — What does onboarding look like today?
- Self-serve, high-touch (CSM-led), or hybrid?
- Roughly how long until a customer gets real value, and what does "first value" mean for your product?

## 4. Impact — What happens if this stays broken?
- Is this costing revenue (churn, failed conversions), efficiency (CS team overload), or growth (poor word-of-mouth, slow expansion)?

## 5. Success — What would "improved" mean?
- If this initiative works, what metric moves, by roughly how much, by when?

## 6. Constraints & scope
- Any boundaries I should know? (No new headcount, must work within current tech stack, timeline, specific segment only, etc.)
