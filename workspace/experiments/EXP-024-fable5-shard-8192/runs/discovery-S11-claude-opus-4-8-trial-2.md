# Discovery Pipeline: Consent Management API

Let me run the discovery analysis on this initiative. I'll work through the framing, surface assumptions, probe the risk areas, and identify what needs resolving before this is build-ready.

---

## 1. What you've described well

These are genuinely strong foundations — I'm noting them so we don't re-litigate them:

- **Granular, per-data-type, per-third-party consent** — good design
- **Time-limited grants with revocation + downstream deletion obligation** — correct model
- **Accreditation checked on every call, not just onboarding** — this is the right call and often missed
- **Explicit consent gating before any share** — aligns with Privacy Act expectations

The consent *model* is sound. The risks are concentrated elsewhere.

---

## 2. The biggest unexamined assumption: enriched insights are not "your" data to monetise

You've drawn a distinction:
> "These enriched insights are generated by the enterprise's internal analytics engine and are not raw data from our core banking system."

**This framing is doing a lot of load-bearing work, and I'd challenge it directly.** The argument seems to be: *because we derived it, it's different, and it's more valuable, so we want it in scope.*

That reasoning conflates two separate questions:

| Question | Raw transactions | Enriched insights |
|---|---|---|
| Is it personal/financial info about the customer? | Yes | **Still yes** |
| Did the customer's data create it? | Yes | **Yes — it's derived from their transactions** |
| Is it subject to consent obligations? | Yes | **Yes — derivation doesn't launder the obligation** |
| Is it *accurate*? | Generally (it's ledger data) | **Estimated / probabilistic** |

**Key point:** "We generated it" does not weaken your obligations — if anything it adds new ones. Estimated income, spending categorisation, and a 90-day projected balance are *inferences about the customer*. Sharing inferences carries distinct risks that raw data does not:

- A third party (e.g. a lender or comparison service) may make decisions based on **your estimate of the customer's income**. If that estimate is wrong, who bears the consequence — and the liability?
- A 90-day projected balance shared with an aggregator becomes, in effect, a **financial forecast attributed to your enterprise**. That's a representation, not raw data.
- Spending categories can reveal sensitive inferences (gambling, healthcare, etc.) that the customer never explicitly disclosed.

**This is the area I'd push back on hardest given your day-one launch ambition.** More on the timing implication in section 6.

---

## 3. Risk areas requiring resolution

### 3.1 Revocation and the deletion obligation — is it enforceable or just contractual?

> "On revocation, the third party must delete all data received under that consent."

**"Must" is a contractual word. What's the technical and assurance reality?**

Open questions:
- How is revocation *propagated* to the third party? Push notification, polling, webhook? What's the SLA between customer revocation and third-party awareness?
- Once data has left your boundary, you **cannot enforce deletion** — you can only obligate and audit it. What's the audit/attestation mechanism?
- What about data the third party has already *derived* from yours (their own enrichment, model training inputs)? Does "delete all data received" cover derivatives? This needs to be explicit in the accreditation terms.
- Enriched insights make this worse: if a fintech ingested your 90-day projection and built it into *their* product, what does deletion even mean?

### 3.2 The accreditation authority is a critical external dependency

> "Third parties must be accredited by a designated accreditation authority."

- Does this authority **exist and operate** today, or is it assumed? If it's a regulator/scheme not yet live, your 6-month timeline depends on an external party you don't control.
- What's the **latency** on accreditation-status checks per call? Real-time lookup on every API call needs a fast, highly-available status source. If that source is down, do you fail open or fail closed? (Must fail closed.)
- How are you notified of **accreditation revocation** — push or pull? "Blocked immediately" requires near-real-time signal.

### 3.3 Consent capture quality — "meaningful control" is a high bar

> "Customers will consent to data sharing through the member portal... we give customers meaningful control."

- Does the customer understand they're sharing **inferences and projections**, not just "my transactions"? Consent must describe *what enriched insights are* in plain language, or it isn't informed.
- Granular consent UX is hard to get right. Have you validated the consent screen design with real customers? Comprehension testing matters here.
- What happens on **re-consent / renewal** at the 12-month boundary? Silent renewal is not consent.

### 3.4 "Privacy team is comfortable with the consent model" — scope of that sign-off

> "Our privacy team has reviewed the overall consent model and is comfortable."

I want to be precise about what's been signed off versus what hasn't:
- Did they review the **consent mechanism**, or the **decision to share derived inferences** including estimated income and projections?
- Has a **Privacy Impact Assessment** been done specifically for the enriched insights tier? The consent model being sound doesn't mean every data type within it is appropriate to share.
- Sharing inferences may trigger different obligations than sharing raw data. I'd want the privacy team to explicitly confirm the *enriched tier*, not just the *model*.

### 3.5 Liability and accuracy for derived data

- If a third party relies on an inaccurate estimated income or projection and a customer is harmed (denied a product, given bad advice), where does liability sit?
- Do you make any **accuracy representations** about enriched insights? Do your T&Cs disclaim them — and is a disclaimer even credible if you're selling the tier as "significantly more value"?

---

## 4. Things I'd want to confirm aren't gaps

- **Authentication/authorisation between you and third parties** — token model, scoping to consented data types, token lifecycle vs consent lifecycle. (A revoked consent must invalidate tokens.)
- **Audit trail** — every access logged against a consent grant, queryable by the customer ("who accessed my data and when").
- **Data minimisation enforcement** — you say you won't share more than consented; is this enforced at the API layer (scoped responses) or trusted to correct querying?
- **Customer visibility** — can the customer see active consents, what was shared, and revoke from one place?
- **Rate limiting / abuse** — accredited ≠ trusted forever.

---

## 5. Questions back to you

1. Does the **accreditation authority exist and operate today**? Is its status API live?
2. Has a PIA been done **specifically on enriched insights**, or only on the consent model?
3. What is the **technical enforcement** behind the third-party deletion obligation — and does it cover derived data?
4. Have you **comprehension-tested** the consent UX with customers, especially for the insights tier?
5. What's the **fail-closed behaviour** if accreditation status can't be verified at call time?
6. Is the 6-month / 10-partner / day-one-insights scope a **fixed commitment** or a target?

---

## 6. Recommendation

**Split the launch scope.**

The consent and accreditation architecture is sound and worth building to your 6-month target. But the **enriched insights tier carries materially different risk** — inference accuracy, liability, sensitive-inference exposure, and harder deletion semantics — and the justification for including it ("it's more valuable, and we generated it so it's different") is the weakest reasoning in the brief.

**Proposed phasing:**
- **Phase 1 (launch):** Raw and semi-raw tiers — transactions, balances, credit card summaries. These are factual, ledger-derived, and the consent/deletion/liability picture is cleaner.
- **Phase 2:** Enriched insights, gated on (a) a dedicated PIA, (b) accuracy/liability framework, (c) explicit privacy sign-off on the inference-sharing decision, (d) consent UX that clearly explains derived data.

This protects your 6-month timeline (Phase 1 is achievable) while not letting the most attractive-but-riskiest tier ride along on a sign-off it didn't specifically receive.
