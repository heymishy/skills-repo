# Discovery Pipeline: Improve Onboarding Experience for New Business Customers

---

## PHASE 1 — REFRAME THE PROBLEM

Before solving anything, let's interrogate the problem statement itself.

### The Statement We Were Given
> *"Improve the onboarding experience for new business customers."*

### What's Ambiguous Here?
| Term | Why It Needs Defining |
|---|---|
| **"Improve"** | By what measure? Speed? Satisfaction? Activation rate? Revenue retention? |
| **"Onboarding"** | First login only? First 30 days? Until first value moment? Until full adoption? |
| **"New business customers"** | SMB vs. enterprise? Self-serve vs. sales-assisted? Industry vertical? |
| **"Experience"** | Product UX? Human touchpoints? Documentation? All of the above? |

### Reframed Problem Options
*(These aren't answers — they're hypotheses to test)*

1. **Activation framing:** New customers aren't reaching their first meaningful value moment quickly enough, leading to early churn.
2. **Confidence framing:** New customers feel uncertain or unsupported during setup, increasing support burden and reducing trust.
3. **Fit framing:** Onboarding assumes a generic customer, but different segments have radically different needs.
4. **Handoff framing:** The transition from sales promise → product reality creates a gap that damages relationships before they start.
5. **Time-to-ROI framing:** Business buyers need to justify the purchase internally — onboarding fails to give them proof points fast enough.

## PHASE 2 — STAKEHOLDER MAP

### Who Has a Stake in This?

```
PRIMARY EXPERIENCING PARTIES
├── The new customer's champion (person who bought it)
├── The new customer's end users (may be different people)
├── The new customer's IT/admin (if technical setup involved)
└── The new customer's executive sponsor (watching for ROI)

YOUR ORGANIZATION
├── Sales team (handoff quality, expectations set)
├── Customer Success / Implementation team
├── Product team (in-product guidance)
├── Support team (ticket volume, early escalations)
├── Marketing (materials, docs, email sequences)
└── Revenue/Finance (retention, expansion revenue)

INDIRECT STAKEHOLDERS
├── Future customers (word of mouth, reviews)
└── Partners/integrators (if they're involved in setup)
```

### Key Tension to Investigate
> The person who **bought** the product ≠ the person who **uses** it ≠ the person who **evaluates renewal**.
> Onboarding often serves only one of these three.

## PHASE 3 — INSIGHT QUESTIONS

*These are research questions, not survey questions. Each one is designed to reveal something that could change your direction.*

### About the Customer's Reality
1. What does a new customer have to do **in their world** before they can even start using your product? (Other systems, approvals, data prep, team buy-in?)
2. What does "ready to go" feel like to them vs. what does it feel like to you?
3. What are they **afraid of** at the moment they start onboarding?
4. What did they tell their boss they'd achieve in the first 90 days?
5. When a customer goes quiet after sign-up, what's actually happening on their end?

### About Failure Modes
6. What does a **failed onboarding** look like? (Churn at day 30? Low adoption? Angry support tickets? Silent disengagement?)
7. Where exactly do customers get stuck — and do they tell you, or do they just stop?
8. What's the most common misconception customers bring into onboarding?
9. Are there customers for whom onboarding works really well? What's different about them?

### About the System
10. What commitments were made during the sales process that onboarding can't deliver on?
11. How does your team know when onboarding has "succeeded"? Is that metric actually meaningful?
12. What does your onboarding assume about the customer that turns out to be wrong?

## PHASE 4 — HYPOTHESES WORTH TESTING

*Ranked by how commonly they explain onboarding failure in B2B contexts — not ranked by likelihood for your specific situation.*

### Hypothesis A — The Value Gap
> Customers don't experience meaningful product value within their expected timeframe, so momentum dies and the investment feels risky.

**Signal that this is true:** Customers who churn early cite "didn't see the value yet" or engagement drops sharply after week 2.

**Signal that this is false:** Customers activate key features quickly but still churn or escalate.

---

### Hypothesis B — The Expectation Mismatch
> Sales sets expectations that onboarding can't match, creating disappointment and eroding trust before the relationship starts.

**Signal that this is true:** Support tickets in weeks 1–3 contain language like "we were told…" or "I thought this would…"

**Signal that this is false:** Customer satisfaction is high through onboarding but drops later, at renewal or expansion.

---

### Hypothesis C — The Overwhelm Problem
> Customers are given too much too soon — too many features, too many emails, too many decisions — and disengage because the cognitive load is too high.

**Signal that this is true:** Customers complete early onboarding steps but dropout rates spike at a specific point; support asks "where do I even start?"

**Signal that this is false:** Customers report they wanted more guidance, not less; they feel under-supported, not overwhelmed.

---

### Hypothesis D — The Wrong Person Problem
> Onboarding is optimized for the buyer/champion, but the actual users — who have different needs, different levels of motivation, and no context on why they're using this — are the ones who disengage.

**Signal that this is true:** Champions report satisfaction but end-user adoption is low; features championed in the sale are never used.

**Signal that this is false:** You can track individual user engagement and it's high across the board, not just for admins.

---

### Hypothesis E — The Resource Bottleneck
> Onboarding is well-designed in theory but your team can't deliver it at scale — response times are slow, meetings are hard to schedule, and customers feel abandoned.

**Signal that this is true:** CSAT is correlated with assigned CSM or account size; enterprise customers succeed, SMB customers don't.

**Signal that this is false:** Self-serve customers perform as well as or better than high-touch customers.

## PHASE 5 — WHAT TO DO NEXT

*In priority order, based on typical discovery maturity.*

### If You Have Almost No Data Yet:
```
IMMEDIATE ACTIONS (this week)
─────────────────────────────
□ Pull your churn/cancellation data and identify:
  - When in the customer lifecycle does churn peak?
  - Is there a cohort pattern (customers from certain segments, 
    channels, or reps churn faster)?

□ Read the last 50 support tickets submitted in the first 30 days
  of a customer relationship. Tag them by theme.

□ Identify 3–5 customers who churned early and 3–5 who became 
  highly successful. Request 20-minute conversations with each.

□ Map your current onboarding steps end-to-end on a whiteboard.
  Mark where you have data vs. where you're guessing.
```

### If You Have Data But No Clear Direction:
```
NEXT ACTIONS
────────────
□ Define "successful onboarding" operationally:
  - What feature(s) must be activated?
  - What action must the customer complete?
  - By what day?

□ Run a cohort analysis: do customers who complete [specific 
  action] in week 1 retain better than those who don't?

□ Map customer-reported pain vs. internally-assumed pain.
  (These are often strikingly different.)
```

### If You Know the Problem and Need to Prioritize Solutions:
```
SOLUTION FRAMING
────────────────
□ For each proposed improvement, ask:
  - Does this reduce time-to-value, or just reduce your cost?
  - Does this serve the buyer, the user, or the evaluator?
  - Can we test this with a small cohort before rolling out?
  - What's the smallest version of this we could ship in 2 weeks?
```

## PHASE 6 — WHAT THIS DISCOVERY PROCESS IS NOT DESIGNED TO DO

*Naming the limits keeps you honest.*

- ❌ This doesn't tell you **which hypothesis is true** for your context. Only research does that.
- ❌ This doesn't replace **talking to actual customers**. Every framework here is a starting point, not a substitute.
- ❌ This doesn't account for **your specific product, market, or team constraints** — those matter enormously.
- ❌ "Improve onboarding" is not a strategy. These phases are designed to help you find **the specific thing worth fixing**, not to validate a pre-decided solution.

---

## SUMMARY — THE CORE QUESTION TO ANSWER FIRST

> **"What is the most common reason a new business customer fails to reach their first moment of genuine value — and what gets in the way of that happening faster?"**

Answer that with evidence, and the right interventions become much more obvious.
