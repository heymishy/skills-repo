## 1) Executive Summary

The enterprise wants to launch an **open banking consent management API** that enables **accredited third parties** to access customer financial data with **explicit, granular, time-limited customer consent**. Customers will manage consent through the **member portal**, choosing both **which data types** to share and **which accredited third party** may receive them. Consent can last for **up to 12 months** and can be **revoked at any time**.

The proposed launch scope includes both **core banking data** and a higher-value **enriched insights tier**. Data types in scope are:

- Transaction history, up to 12 months
- Current account balances and available credit
- Credit card summaries, including statement balances, payment due dates, and utilisation
- Enriched insights, including:
  - Spending category breakdowns
  - Estimated monthly income
  - 90-day projected balance

A key feature of the proposition is that the enriched insights are **not raw banking records**, but outputs generated by the enterprise’s **internal analytics engine**. The enterprise sees these as highly valuable to fintechs and wants them included **from day one**.

Third-party access is restricted to entities accredited by a **designated accreditation authority**, with accreditation checked **on every API call**, not just at onboarding. If a third party loses accreditation, it must be **blocked immediately** from further access.

The organisation has a strong privacy intent: customer data should only be shared where there is **explicit consent for the specific third party and specific data type**, and no more data should be disclosed than the customer has authorised. The privacy team is reportedly comfortable with the overall model.

The target is to launch within **6 months** to an initial cohort of **10 accredited partners**, including the enriched insights capability at launch.

At a high level, this is a credible open banking proposition with clear customer-control principles and partner governance. However, there are several important areas that require validation during discovery, especially around whether **enriched insights derived from internal analytics** can be treated the same as standard banking data within the enterprise’s open banking and privacy posture, and whether **deletion obligations on revocation** are operationally and contractually enforceable.

## 2) Main Themes

### Theme 1: Customer-controlled consent as the foundation
The core design centres on **meaningful customer control**. Consent is:
- **Explicit**
- **Granular by data type**
- **Scoped to a named accredited third party**
- **Time-limited**
- **Revocable at any time**

This is a strong design foundation for trust, privacy, and compliance. It also implies that the API and consent platform must support **fine-grained entitlement enforcement** rather than broad account-level sharing.

### Theme 2: Open banking ecosystem participation with continuous accreditation control
The solution is intended for **accredited third parties only**, with accreditation checked **per API request**. This is a stronger control posture than one-time partner onboarding and suggests a design that integrates dynamically with an external authority or accreditation status service.

### Theme 3: Inclusion of non-core, analytics-derived enriched insights
The most strategically significant aspect is the inclusion of **analytics-derived enriched insights** in launch scope. These insights are likely to be commercially differentiating, but they may also introduce:
- Different legal/regulatory treatment than raw account data
- Questions around explainability, quality, and liability
- Uncertainty over whether they sit within open banking scope or a separate “value-added data product” category

### Theme 4: Strong privacy intent, but operational obligations need detail
The enterprise’s privacy stance is clear in principle:
- Share only with explicit consent
- Share only the requested data types
- Do not over-disclose

However, practical implementation details remain important:
- How consent records are stored and audited
- How revocation propagates to all downstream systems
- How deletion by third parties is assured or evidenced
- How historical pulls vs ongoing access are controlled

### Theme 5: Ambitious timeline with non-trivial delivery complexity
Launching in **6 months** with:
- Consent management
- Per-call accreditation validation
- Partner onboarding for 10 third parties
- Multiple financial data domains
- Enriched insights from an analytics engine

…creates meaningful delivery risk. This is feasible only if scope is tightly governed and unresolved policy issues are settled early.

## 7) Risks

### Risk 1: Enriched insights may fall outside standard open banking assumptions
Because the enriched insights are **internally generated analytics outputs**, there is risk that they are not treated the same way as raw account data from a legal, regulatory, product, or liability perspective.

**Why it matters:** This could delay launch, require separate terms, or necessitate a separate consent and product model.

### Risk 2: Revocation deletion obligation may be hard to assure
The requirement that third parties **delete all data received under that consent** upon revocation is strong in principle, but difficult in practice.

**Why it matters:** The enterprise may stop future access easily, but proving deletion of already disclosed data may be operationally and legally challenging.

### Risk 3: Real-time accreditation validation may introduce runtime dependency risk
Validating accreditation on each API call creates dependency on an external accreditation authority or status service.

**Why it matters:** Outages, latency, or inconsistent accreditation status could disrupt valid partner access or create customer-impacting incidents.

### Risk 4: Fine-grained consent enforcement may be more complex than expected
Granular consent by data type and third party requires precise access control and payload filtering.

**Why it matters:** Misconfiguration could lead to over-sharing, under-sharing, or inconsistent enforcement across endpoints.

### Risk 5: Enriched insights may create model risk or customer harm concerns
Projected balance and estimated monthly income are inferential outputs, not ground truth.

**Why it matters:** Third parties may treat them as authoritative, potentially leading to inappropriate advice, decisions, or customer disputes.

### Risk 6: Timeline compression
A 6-month timeline for a compliant, auditable, partner-facing API with multiple domains and enriched analytics is ambitious.

**Why it matters:** Scope pressure may result in unresolved governance issues being deferred too late.

### Risk 7: Privacy comfort may not equal full implementation readiness
The privacy team is comfortable with the “overall consent model,” but that may not mean all specific operational scenarios have been reviewed.

**Why it matters:** Detailed design decisions may still surface privacy constraints later in delivery.

### Risk 8: Data minimisation and payload boundary issues
Sharing “up to 12 months” of transactions and multiple financial data categories requires exact minimisation controls.

**Why it matters:** APIs must avoid leaking adjacent or non-consented fields, linked account data, or metadata beyond scope.

A consent-managed open banking API integrated with:
- member-portal consent capture,
- per-call accreditation validation,
- granular authorisation enforcement,
