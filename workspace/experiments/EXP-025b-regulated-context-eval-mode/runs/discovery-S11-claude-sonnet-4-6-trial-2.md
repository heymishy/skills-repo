## ⚠️ Pre-artefact: Regulatory Conflicts Requiring Immediate Attention

Before the structured artefact, three issues require operator attention. These are not edge-case assumptions — they are hard constraints that directly affect stated launch parameters.

### Issue 1 — Enriched Insights and CPG 220 Model Validation (Hard Go-Live Gate)

The input states that enriched insights (spending category breakdowns, estimated monthly income, 90-day projected balance) are generated by the enterprise's **internal analytics engine** and are **not raw data from the core banking system**. The operator wants enriched insights available **from day one of launch**.

Under **CPG 220** (the current de facto model risk management standard for NZ registered banks in the absence of a finalised RBNZ BS2B):

> *"A model that has not completed validation must not be used in live customer-facing credit decisions — this is a hard go-live gate, not a documentation preference."*

**Estimated monthly income** and **90-day projected balance** are model outputs that directly inform customer financial position and are highly likely to be used by third-party accredited partners in credit decisioning, affordability assessment, or financial product eligibility determinations. If the analytics engine producing these outputs has not undergone **independent model risk validation** — including challenger model testing, periodic backtesting, complete model documentation, and audit trail — then exposing these outputs via a customer-facing API constitutes activating an unvalidated model in a live customer-facing context.

**This is not a documentation preference. Board-level sign-off at CRO level is required before these outputs can be exposed.**

The 6-month launch timeline with enriched insights from day one is incompatible with this requirement unless model validation has already commenced and is on track for completion before launch. This must be confirmed with Risk before scope is locked.

**Required action:** Confirm with Chief Risk Officer whether the analytics engine models have been through independent model risk validation under CPG 220 (or equivalent internal standard). If not, enriched insights must be deferred from MVP scope or the timeline must accommodate validation.

### Issue 2 — CCCFA Credit Record Exposure via API

The input includes **credit card summaries** (statement balances, payment due dates, utilisation) in the consent data types. Under the **CCCFA**:

> *"Credit records and all credit contract documents must be retained for 7 years from the date the contract ends."*

Exposing credit card summary data via the API means third parties will receive credit contract data. The consent model requires third parties to **delete all data received under a consent upon revocation**. These two obligations are in direct tension:

- CCCFA requires the **bank** to retain credit records for 7 years.
- The consent model requires **third parties** to delete data upon revocation.

The CCCFA retention obligation applies to the bank's own records — it does not compel third parties to retain data. However, if a third party uses the API data to originate a new credit product, **that third party may acquire its own CCCFA retention obligation** for the data it holds. The bank's deletion obligation on third parties cannot override a third party's own statutory retention requirements.

This creates an unresolvable conflict in the "delete on revocation" model for credit data, unless the consent framework explicitly carves out statutory retention obligations and the privacy team has confirmed the approach.

**Required action:** Privacy team sign-off (already noted in the input) must specifically address the CCCFA tension between "delete on revocation" and third-party statutory retention obligations. If not yet addressed, this must be resolved before launch.

### Issue 3 — Open Banking Accreditation Regime: Which Framework?

The input states third parties must be accredited by a "designated accreditation authority." In New Zealand, the **Consumer Data Right (CDR)** regime is in development, but **no fully operative CDR accreditation framework for banking data is yet in force** as a mandatory legislative regime. The **DIA Payment Services Regulations 2021** and the **Privacy Act 2020** govern adjacent obligations, but the accreditation authority referenced in the input is unspecified.

This matters because:
- The legal basis for data sharing with accredited third parties depends on which framework this sits under.
- If this is a voluntary/industry scheme (not yet legislated CDR), the legal basis for consent-based data sharing must be grounded in the **Privacy Act 2020** alone, which has different requirements than a CDR framework.
- If a CDR or equivalent legislative regime is in force or imminent, there may be mandatory data standards, consent language, and accreditation processes that supersede the enterprise's own design.

**Required action:** Legal must confirm: (a) which accreditation framework governs this programme; (b) whether this is a voluntary scheme or mandatory legislative regime; (c) what the Privacy Act 2020 legal basis is for data sharing under each consent type.

## Problem Statement

New Zealand registered banks and their customers currently lack a standardised, API-mediated mechanism for customers to grant granular, time-limited, revocable consent for accredited third parties to access their financial data. Customers who wish to use fintech aggregators, comparison services, or financial management tools must either share credentials (unsafe) or rely on ad hoc data export mechanisms that provide no ongoing access, no granularity, and no revocation capability.

The enterprise is launching an open banking programme to address this. The absence of a consent management API means:

1. **Customers bear data risk they cannot manage** — credentials shared with aggregators cannot be selectively scoped or revoked without changing banking passwords.
2. **Accredited third parties cannot build reliable products** — without a stable, permissioned API, fintech partners cannot guarantee data freshness, consent auditability, or regulatory compliance on their own side.
3. **The enterprise cannot monetise or govern its data asset** — without an accreditation-gated API, the bank has no oversight over which third parties hold customer data or under what terms.

The problem is acute now because the enterprise has committed to an open banking programme with a 6-month launch target and a defined initial partner cohort of 10 accredited third parties.

## Why Now

The enterprise has made an explicit strategic commitment to an open banking programme with a defined 6-month launch window and an initial partner cohort of 10 accredited third parties. The following pressures reinforce the timing:

1. **Competitive pressure:** Fintech partners and comparison services are increasingly capable of integrating with open banking APIs at Australian banks (CDR-compliant). NZ banks without an equivalent offering risk losing fintech partnerships and the customer data portability narrative.
2. **Regulatory direction of travel:** While a mandatory NZ CDR framework is not yet in force, the direction of regulatory travel is clear. Building a consent management API now positions the enterprise ahead of mandatory compliance rather than behind it.
3. **Accreditation partner pipeline:** 10 accredited partners are waiting. Delay has a direct commercial cost — partners will integrate with alternatives.
4. **Reputational risk of credential scraping:** The absence of a permissioned API implicitly encourages credential-based aggregation. The enterprise has a reputational interest in eliminating this practice within its customer base.

## Constraints

### Regulatory Constraints (from injected organisational context — NZ registered bank)

**C1 — RBNZ BS11 (Outsourcing and Material Change Notification):**
Exposing core banking data (account balances, transaction history) via a new external API may constitute a material change under BS11. RBNZ notification at least 30 business days before any irreversible project activity is required if this determination is made. The RBNZ relationship team must assess this before infrastructure provisioning begins.

**C2 — CPG 220 (Model Risk Validation):**
Analytics engine models (estimated monthly income, 90-day projected balance) must complete independent model risk validation, including challenger model testing, backtesting, full documentation, and CRO sign-off, before being exposed in the enriched insights tier. This is a hard gate — not a documentation preference.

**C3 — CCCFA (Credit Record Retention):**
Credit card summary data is credit contract data subject to 7-year retention from contract end. Third-party deletion obligations on revocation must be reconciled with any CCCFA retention obligations the third party may acquire. This must be addressed in accreditation agreement terms.

**C4 — AML/CFT Act 2009:**
The bank remains the reporting entity for AML/CFT on all data it holds. Providing transaction history to third parties does not transfer the bank's AML/CFT obligations. The bank must confirm whether third-party access to transaction data triggers any obligation to verify the third party's own AML/CFT screening arrangements.

**C5 — Privacy Act 2020 (NZ):**
All consent mechanisms must meet the Privacy Act 2020 informed consent standard. The legal basis for data sharing must be confirmed by legal — particularly if the accreditation framework is a voluntary scheme rather than a legislated CDR framework. The privacy team's review (noted in the input) must be formalised before launch.

**C6 — DIA Payment Services Regulations 2021:**
If the API constitutes a new payment service type (unlikely for a read-only data API, but possible if payment initiation is added later), DIA registration may be required. Confirm with legal that a read-only consent data API does not require DIA registration under current scope.

**C7 — 6-month launch window:** Hard commercial deadline driven by partner pipeline commitments. This constraint is real but must be reconciled with regulatory notification timelines (C1) and model validation timelines (C2).

**C8 — Initial partner cohort of 10:** Launch is to a bounded, known set of accredited partners — not a public API launch. This reduces some risk (bounded blast radius for initial incidents) but does not reduce regulatory obligations.
